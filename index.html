Academic journals

Three major journals you'll encounter as a CS major. Learning to read and cite these early puts you ahead.

Journal of the ACM (JACM)

Publisher: Association for Computing Machinery  ·  Founded: 1954

JACM is one of the oldest and most respected journals in the field. It publishes foundational, peer-reviewed research across all areas of computer science including algorithms, theory, programming languages, and systems. As a CS student, you'll likely encounter papers from JACM in upper-level courses and independent research. It is the gold standard for theoretical CS.

TheoryAlgorithmsSystems

IEEE Transactions on Pattern Analysis and Machine Intelligence (TPAMI)

Publisher: IEEE Computer Society  ·  2024 Impact Factor: 18.6

TPAMI is the highest-ranked AI journal by impact factor as of 2024. It covers machine learning, computer vision, and pattern recognition, areas that are increasingly central to CS coursework and industry hiring. Howard's research in AI and cybersecurity, led by Prof. Danda Rawat at the DSC2 center, connects directly to the topics published here.

AI / MLComputer visionPattern recognition

IEEE Transactions on Software Engineering (TSE)

Publisher: IEEE Computer Society  ·  Focus: Software design, testing, maintenance

TSE covers the science and practice of building software, including architecture, testing methodologies, development processes, and security. Whether you're heading into industry or research, this journal reflects the problems you'll be solving. The methods taught in Howard's software engineering courses trace back to research published in venues like TSE.

Key critical terms

10 terms you need to know. These come up in classes, research, and job interviews.

Algorithm

A step-by-step set of instructions for solving a problem. The backbone of CS, every program runs on one.

Time complexity

How the runtime of an algorithm scales as input size grows. Usually written in Big-O notation (e.g., O(n log n)).

Data structure

A way of organizing data in memory (arrays, linked lists, trees, graphs). Choosing the right one can make or break a program.

Abstraction

Hiding complex details behind a simpler interface. It's how we manage complexity in large software systems.

Recursion

When a function calls itself to solve a smaller version of the same problem. Common in sorting, tree traversal, and parsing.

Cybersecurity

Protecting systems and networks from attacks. A major research focus at Howard through the DSC2 center led by Prof. Rawat.

Machine learning

Training algorithms to learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed. Drives AI applications everywhere.

Operating system

Software that manages hardware resources and provides services to programs. You'll study these deeply in systems courses.

API

Application Programming Interface. A contract that lets different software components talk to each other. You'll use these constantly.

Version control

Tracking and managing changes to code over time. Git is the industry standard and you'll use it from day one.

Undergraduate organizations

Two orgs you should know about from the start. Both have active chapters at Howard and can open serious doors.

National Society of Black Engineers (NSBE) — Howard Chapter

Type: Professional student organization  ·  Founded nationally: 1975, Purdue University

NSBE was created to represent the interests of Black and minority engineers and CS students. The Howard chapter runs technical writing workshops, round-table discussions on engineering developments, and participates in the Region II conference. They distribute a newsletter with job market analyses and grad school information. If you're serious about networking and finding internships or full-time roles at major tech companies, NSBE is where you start. Activities have included participation in the Black Engineer of the Year Awards (BEYA) STEM Conference.

NetworkingJob placementCommunity

Upsilon Pi Epsilon (UPE) — Beta Chapter of Washington DC

Type: Honor society  ·  Howard chapter chartered: 2004  ·  Founded: 1967, Texas A&M University

UPE is the only international honor society dedicated exclusively to computing and information disciplines. Membership is by invitation only, based on academic excellence. It is endorsed by both ACM and the IEEE Computer Society and is a member of the Association of College Honor Societies (ACHS). The Howard chapter was chartered in 2004 as the Beta Chapter of Washington DC. Getting into UPE signals to employers and graduate programs that you are among the top students in your class. The society also presents scholarship awards and participates in the ACM International Scholastic Programming Contest.

Job opportunities

Three careers your CS degree prepares you for, with real job listings to browse.

Software engineer

Avg. entry-level salary: $110,000–$160,000  ·  Demand: Very high

The most common path for CS graduates. Software engineers design, build, and maintain applications and systems. At large companies you may specialize in frontend, backend, or full-stack development. At smaller companies you'll likely do all three. Howard CS graduates have gone on to work at companies ranging from startups to Google, government research labs, and federal agencies. The B.S. curriculum at Howard is designed specifically to prepare you for these roles.

Cybersecurity analyst

Avg. salary: $95,000–$140,000  ·  Demand: Very high, especially in Washington DC

Cybersecurity analysts protect organizations from data breaches, malware, and cyberattacks. Given Howard's location in DC and its NSF- and NNSA-funded Data Science and Cybersecurity Center (DSC2), this is a particularly strong path for Howard CS graduates. Federal agencies including NSA, CISA, and the Department of Defense are major local employers. Howard has received a $3 million NNSA grant specifically for cybersecurity training.

Browse federal cybersecurity jobs on USAJobs ↗

Machine learning engineer

Avg. salary: $120,000–$180,000  ·  Demand: Rapidly growing

ML engineers build the systems that train and deploy AI models. This role sits at the intersection of software engineering and data science. Howard recently partnered with CodePath and the Thurgood Marshall College Fund to integrate industry-aligned AI training directly into its Intro to AI course, creating a direct pipeline into roles at leading tech companies.

Formatting conventions

Computer Science primarily uses IEEE and ACM citation styles. Here's what you need to know before you write your first technical paper.

IEEE style (most common in CS)

Used in most technical papers, especially in engineering and systems courses. Citations appear as numbered references in brackets: [1], [2], etc. The reference list appears at the end, numbered in order of first citation.

Journal article

A. Lastname, "Title of paper," Journal Name, vol. X, no. Y, pp. ZZ–ZZ, Month Year.

Book

B. Author, Book Title. City: Publisher, Year, pp. ZZ–ZZ.

Conference paper

C. Author, "Paper title," in Proc. Conf. Name, City, Year, pp. ZZ–ZZ.

ACM style (used in computing-specific publications)

Used by the Association for Computing Machinery in its journals and conferences. ACM uses a numbered format, similar to IEEE in recent editions, though author-date variants still appear in some publications.

Journal article (ACM)

Firstname Lastname. Year. Title of article. Journal Name Vol, Issue (Month Year), Pages. DOI.
Quick tip: When in doubt, ask your professor which style they prefer. For independent research or club publications, IEEE is the safe default in computer science.
